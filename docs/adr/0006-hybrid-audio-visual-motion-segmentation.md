# Hybrid audio + visual-motion segmentation

Rally/gap segmentation uses **visual motion as the primary boundary signal, with audio hit-density as confirmation**. Motion energy from frame-to-frame differencing decides where a rally starts and ends; audio onsets decide whether a moving span is actually a rally. This revises the **audio-first** stance of ADR 0002 — local, GPU-free, heuristic-not-learned, replaceable behind the timeline, per-region confidence, and inclusion bias all still hold; only the dominant signal changes.

Audio alone proved insufficient for boundaries in practice, even after extensive tuning. Audio is good at "a hit happened" but rally **boundaries are semantic, not acoustic**: a rally is preceded by shuttle-bouncing, talking, and the serve routine (all of which sound like activity), and its end is "last hit, then quiet" — acoustically identical to a mid-rally lull. Add neighbouring-court bleed (a limitation ADR 0002 already named) and there is not enough signal in audio to place edges reliably. The observed failure spanned all modes: imprecise edges, missed/merged rallies, false positives, and poor generalisation across recordings.

Visual motion carries the signal audio lacks. A rally **starts** with explosive, whole-court movement (the receiver exploding off the serve) and **ends** when both players decelerate after the point — so the motion envelope's rising and falling edges land where the rally boundaries actually are. Motion is computed cheaply and locally by decoding **downscaled, low-frame-rate grayscale frames** (via the ffmpeg sidecar, ADR 0004) and taking the mean absolute difference between consecutive frames; no GPU, no learned model, no optical flow. The result is one motion-energy value per sampled frame, aggregated onto the same analysis-block grid as the audio.

The two signals fuse with a clear division of labour: **motion defines the candidate play spans and their boundaries; audio confirms each span is a rally and not non-rally movement** (players walking together to collect shuttles, towelling off, warming up). A motion-active run is kept only if it carries enough shuttle-hit onsets — hit *count* over a multi-second span is robust even though hit *timing* is not, which is exactly the property audio can still contribute. This also turns audio's neighbour-court weakness into a non-issue: a neighbouring court is out of frame, so it never drives motion, and on-court non-rally motion is filtered by the audio-confirmation step.

**Assumption, documented not enforced: the camera is roughly static** (tripod or propped), the norm for self-recorded court video. Frame-differencing measures motion only under a fixed frame; a panning or handheld camera would register global motion everywhere and break the signal, and would need optical flow or global-motion compensation instead — out of scope. Other constraints carried over: segmentation now decodes video frames as well as audio, a heavier one-time background pass than audio-only, but still local and off the playback path. Thresholds remain a tunable [`Params`] block adjusted by a human against real footage (the HITL loop; see `docs/tuning-segmentation.md`), and the segmenter stays a replaceable component — a future learned model is still a contained swap behind the timeline.

Supersedes the audio-first stance of ADR 0002 (issue #3). The rest of ADR 0002 stands.
